Currently the UNESCO has enlisted 48 sites in its danger list.

The list is curated by a comittee that defines the reason for endangerment with detailed reports and articles.

The archeological site of Palmyra, situated in Syria in the Arab States region, is recently added to the danger list.

An oasis in the Syrian desert, north-east of Damascus, Palmyra contains the monumental ruins of a great city that was one of the most important cultural centres of the ancient world.

Its track go back as far as the 2nd millenium before Christ.

As a result of the Syrian Civil War, Palmyra experienced widespread looting and damage by combatants.

On 13 May 2015, ISIL launched an attack on the modern town of Tadmur, sparking fears that the iconoclastic group would destroy the adjacent ancient site of Palmyra.

The site of Palmyra is:
“The legacy of all civilization not only of Syria but all mankind. What will happen in Syria will the determine the fate of civilization itself.”

Information becomes history almost immediately online, compiling to a massive collection of time-tagged, searchable materials that document our present/past.

Does this massive accumulation create a more complete representation of the present/past? Where does real-time/world actuality diverge from what is circulating in our media?

Investigating our most common search engine, google images acts as a tool to access information about the visual world quickly.

We seek to understand how closely this visual history resembles actuality in a case study for the UNESCO World Heritage site of Palmyra.

In a time-based study, we look at the top 100 image results for “Palmyra” in 2010 compared with 2016.

We believe this use of imagery can produce emotional response from our audience, sensibilizing the impact of humans and time on the persistence of its heritage.
